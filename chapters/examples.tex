\section{Citations and Paragraph Formatting}
\label{sec:citations}

\subsection{In-Text Citation Examples}

The following examples demonstrate common citation patterns using the
APA style.

\textbf{Parenthetical citations} — the most common form:
several studies have investigated deep learning for image recognition
\parencite{chen2023survey}.  Convolutional neural networks remain the
dominant architecture in this domain \parencite{liu2024cnn}, though
recent work suggests alternatives \parencite{example2024}.

\textbf{Narrative citations} — the author's name appears as
part of the sentence: \textcite{examplebook2023} provided
a comprehensive overview of academic writing conventions.  More
recently, \textcite{chen2023survey} surveyed over 200 papers on
natural language processing applications.  In her doctoral work,
\textcite{kim2023dissertation} developed novel computational methods
for genomic analysis.

\textbf{Multiple citations together}: prior work has
addressed this problem from several angles \parencite{example2024,
chen2023survey,liu2024cnn}.  When citing multiple sources, APA style
orders them alphabetically and separates with semicolons.

\textbf{Citing specific parts}: gradient-based methods are widely
adopted \parencite[see][chap.\ 3]{patel2024optimisation}, and the
implementation details are well-documented
\parencite{moore2024documentation}.  Technical standards also provide
guidance on data formats \parencite{ietf2024}.

\textbf{Year-only and page citations}: the foundational work appeared
in \citeyear{examplebook2023}, and later extensions can be found in
\textcite[p.~45]{wang2024thesis}.

\subsection{Paragraph Spacing and Indentation}

The following two paragraphs demonstrate the default paragraph
formatting in this template.  Paragraphs are separated by vertical
space (1.5-line spacing throughout) with no first-line indentation,
as commonly used in theses following UoA guidelines.

The advent of deep learning has fundamentally transformed the
landscape of artificial intelligence research over the past decade.
Convolutional neural networks \parencite{liu2024cnn}, recurrent
architectures, and more recently transformer-based models have
achieved state-of-the-art performance across a wide range of tasks
including computer vision, natural language processing, and speech
recognition.  This rapid progress has been driven by the convergence
of several key factors: the availability of large-scale annotated
datasets, the development of powerful graphics processing units for
parallel computation, and algorithmic innovations in training deep
networks.  The impact of these advances extends beyond academic
research into industrial applications, where deep learning systems
now power everything from recommendation engines to autonomous
vehicles.

Despite these remarkable achievements, significant challenges remain.
Modern deep learning models require enormous computational resources
for training, raising concerns about energy consumption and
environmental impact \parencite{chen2023survey}.  Moreover, these
models often function as black boxes, making it difficult to
interpret their decisions or to guarantee their reliability in
safety-critical applications.  Researchers have proposed various
approaches to address these limitations, including model compression
techniques \parencite{wang2024thesis}, interpretability methods, and
the integration of domain knowledge through hybrid architectures
\parencite{patel2024optimisation}.  Addressing these challenges is
essential for the responsible deployment of AI systems in healthcare,
transportation, and other domains where errors can have serious
consequences.

\section{Mathematical Equations}
\label{sec:equations}

\subsection{Inline Equation}

The well-known mass--energy equivalence is $E = mc^2$, where $m$ is
the rest mass and $c$ is the speed of light in vacuum.

\subsection{Display Equation (Unnumbered)}

The gravitational force between two masses is given by:
\[
  F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}
\]
where $G$ is the gravitational constant.

\subsection{Numbered Equation (Cross-Referenced)}

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n}
    \bigl( y_i - f(x_i; \theta) \bigr)^2
  \label{eq:mse}
\end{equation}
Equation~\ref{eq:mse} defines the mean-squared-error loss function
commonly used in regression tasks.

\subsection{Multi-Line Aligned Equations}

\begin{align}
  y &= \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \varepsilon
     \label{eq:linear_model} \\
  \hat{\beta} &= (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y
     \label{eq:ols}
\end{align}
Equations~\ref{eq:linear_model} and~\ref{eq:ols} show the linear
regression model and its ordinary-least-squares solution.

\subsection{Case-Based Definition}

\begin{equation}
  \text{ReLU}(x) =
  \begin{cases}
    x,  & x \geq 0 \\
    0,  & x < 0
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:relu}
\end{equation}
Equation~\ref{eq:relu} defines the Rectified Linear Unit activation function.

\section{Algorithms / Pseudocode}
\label{sec:algorithms}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:gradient_descent} demonstrates a simple gradient
descent optimisation procedure using the algorithm2e package.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
  \caption{Gradient Descent Optimisation}
  \label{alg:gradient_descent}
  \SetKwInput{KwParams}{Parameters}
  \KwParams{Learning rate $\eta$, max iter.\ $T$}
  \SetKwInput{KwInit}{Initialise}
  \KwInit{Model parameters $\theta_0$}
  \For{$t \leftarrow 1$ \KwTo $T$}{
    Compute gradient $\nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_{t-1})$\;
    Update $\theta_t \leftarrow \theta_{t-1} - \eta \nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_{t-1})$\;
        \label{line:update}
    \If{$\|\nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_t)\| < \epsilon$}{
      \textbf{break}\;
    }
  }
  \Return{$\theta_t$}\;
\end{algorithm}

Line~\ref{line:update} of Algorithm~\ref{alg:gradient_descent}
performs the standard parameter update step.
